# ERA — The Living World: Interwoven Lives (the same rails)

**Status: PROPOSED.** Per Roy: all the living must *interact*, in a town, **on
the same rails** — lives bond, age and are lost across years; tens of lives
meeting on different days and occasions; **scripted for years.** This is the
connective system that binds [`residents.md`](residents.md) and
[`places.md`](places.md) into one living town rather than a set of parallel
routines.

> The **decision** to realize this by simulating *intention* (not movement) is
> [`CD-006`](../creative-decisions/CD-006-simulate-intention-not-movement.md); its
> **technical strategy** (build-vs-buy, the Town Engine, a six-week vertical slice)
> is [`town-engine-technical-strategy.md`](../research/town-engine-technical-strategy.md).
> Both PROPOSED.

---

## 1. The principle

The district is **one web of interacting lives on a single shared timeline.**
Lives meet, grow fond, drift, quarrel, care for one another, age, and are lost.
The player only ever witnesses *fragments* over years — the whole is always
larger than any session, and larger than the player.

This is where the living world stops being scenery and becomes a place you can
*know*. You come to know these threads the way you know real neighbours:
incompletely, over time, by recurrence.

Philosophically it is the payoff of everything beneath it:
- **A theatre without an audience** — lives interact *for each other*, never for
  the player.
- **The world continues without the player** — the web runs whether or not anyone
  is watching (IP-002 Independence / axiom AR).
- **Living Memory and the social substrate** — because the lives are
  *co-witnessed on shared rails*, they become the district's collective memory,
  not private nostalgia (IP-002 Recognition / A2).
- **Honest history** — bonds end, animals die, people leave; the losses are real
  and leave traces (IP-001 AT-7; the four textures of loss, IP-002).

---

## 2. The same rails (one shared clock)

Every life runs on **one persistent, shared timeline.** The same day is the same
day for everyone, so:

- Interactions are **consistent** — the boy looks for the dog on the *same*
  mornings the dog is (or isn't) on the bakery step, for every player and every
  return.
- The world is **rememberable and speakable** — "the kid always looks for the dog
  by the bakery" is *true*, so it can become something the district (and the
  player) holds as memory.
- Threads are **co-witnessed** — other lives can see the same events, which is
  what makes them count as shared history rather than a private scripted moment.

The rails are the difference between a world that *loops* and a world that *has a
past.*

---

## 3. The levels of life

The web spans three scales, all on the same rails:

- **Named residents** — the authored cast (the Old Dog, the Twins, Agnes, Victor…
  see `residents.md`).
- **Minor residents** — the "tens of lives": more townsfolk with lighter
  definitions (a face at a window, a paperboy, a dog-walker, a market trader).
- **Micro-life** — squirrels, birds, cats, foxes, insects: ambient, but **able to
  be drawn into interactions** (the dog and the squirrels; the cats that vanish
  when the dog arrives; the riverside fox).

A named dog and a wild squirrel belong to the same web.

---

## 4. The web: how lives interact

Relationships are **edges** between lives. Each edge has a **type**, a **strength
that evolves over time**, and **encounter rules** (how often, and under what
conditions of place, time, season, weather, match day, and institution state):

- **Bonds** — a growing fondness (the boy and the dog; Hana and the Old Dog).
- **Chance encounters** — the *"sometimes"* of real life: probabilistic meetings
  scheduled across the timeline, not every day (sometimes the boy finds the dog;
  sometimes he doesn't).
- **Care** — Eva leaves flowers at Agnes's bench; Hana mothers Luca.
- **Rivalries & tensions** — the cats and the dog; the Twins and Elias's rake.
- **Romance** — Daniel & Sofia, over years.
- **Cross-species** — the dog and the squirrels; the fox and the night.

The edges are what make tens of lives feel like a town instead of a crowd.

---

## 5. Scripted for years: arcs & aging

Each life carries an **authored arc of milestones across years** (the skeleton)
expressed through **emergent daily texture** (the flesh):

- Lives **age on the timeline**: the Old Dog grows slower and greyer and one day
  is gone; the boy grows up; one of the Twins walks into the academy; Agnes's
  visits thin and then stop; Daniel & Sofia marry.
- Milestones are **authored** (the dog will age and be lost around a set point;
  the boy's bond will deepen and then transform into grief and memory), so the
  arcs have shape and meaning.
- Daily expression is **emergent/varied** (which mornings the boy comes; whether
  he finds the dog today; whether the dog is chasing squirrels or asleep in the
  sun), so it never feels canned.

This is the agency–otherness balance (IP-002 AR) applied to *authorship of the
world*: authored where meaning lives, emergent where texture lives. **AI
orchestrates the daily variation within the authored bounds** — multiplying
authored richness, not outsourcing it (Design Bible Vol VII) — and every
generated beat must pass the **Human Test** (would a real neighbour believe this
could naturally happen?).

## 6. Worked example — the boy and the Old Dog  _[CKO-proposed]_

Roy's example, developed as the canonical illustration of the whole system.
*(Minor resident — a small boy from the riverside houses; provisional name
**Tomas**, open to change.)*

**The bond (a season).** Tomas starts noticing the Old Dog on the bakery step on
his way past. At first he just watches. Then he brings a corner of Hana's roll.
By winter the dog lifts its head when Tomas comes.

**The "sometimes" (years, on the rails).** On *some* mornings Tomas comes looking
for the dog — not every day, and not on command. Sometimes the dog is on the
step and they sit a while. Sometimes the dog isn't there and Tomas checks the
café shade, the plaza, the riverside, and gives up. The player might catch this
once and not again for months — and both times it will be *true*, because it's on
the same rails.

**The squirrels (cross-species texture).** The dog and the riverside squirrels
run a slow, low-stakes rivalry — the dog gives chase, never catches one, and
Tomas laughs. The squirrels are micro-life pulled into the web.

**Aging (scripted for years).** Across seasons the dog slows: stiffer in winter,
longer sleeps, greyer muzzle. Tomas grows too — taller, then too old to sit on
the step, then a teenager who still slows as he passes.

**The loss (authored milestone; four textures at once).** One winter the dog is
simply gone. Hana's first roll sits untouched. It is **Tomas** who notices first,
and leaves something on the bakery step. The whole district feels it without
being told. This single loss strikes all four textures (IP-002 derived model):
the *place* (the empty step), *regard* (the one who searched for him), *narrative*
(the thread that ran for years), and the *uncontrollable* (age, which no one
could stop). It is never undone.

That one thread demonstrates the entire system: shared rails · a bond that
evolves · chance "sometimes" encounters · cross-species micro-life · multi-year
aging · authored milestone + emergent daily texture · a compounding, honored
loss · and a life (Tomas) changed by having witnessed another.

## 7. How the player experiences it

- The player sees **fragments**, months apart, and assembles meaning *below the
  level of any single session* — the "fifty things happening that have nothing to
  do with you," now connected to each other.
- The player is **never the cause** and never the completer — there are no quests,
  no triggers, no "help the boy find the dog" task. The threads run regardless.
- But the world **may remember what the player witnessed** — a light-touch
  recognition (IP-002 A2): the district can acknowledge, later and unprompted,
  the morning the player happened to be there for the dog's last.

---

## 8. Authoring model (making tens of lives tractable)

So this stays buildable rather than a promise the game can't keep:

- **Per-life:** a timeline of authored beats (milestones) + daily behaviour rules
  (the routines already in `residents.md`).
- **Relationships:** a graph of edges (type · evolving strength · encounter
  rules).
- **Scheduler:** encounters resolved probabilistically against the *shared clock*,
  conditioned on place/time/season/weather/match-day/state — deterministic enough
  that the same day is the same day (consistency), varied enough to feel alive.
- **AI orchestration:** selects and varies daily texture *within* authored bounds;
  Human Test is the acceptance bar.
- **Scale:** the "tens of lives" are a bounded graph plus a scheduler — richness
  comes from the *combinations*, not from hand-animating every day.

---

## 9. Never

- Lives interact **for each other**, never perform for the player.
- **No quests or triggers** — the player never starts or finishes a thread.
- **Losses are honored, never undone** (no resurrecting the dog; no rewind).
- **Authored at milestones, emergent in texture** — never fully random (never
  feels canned), never fully scripted (never feels dead).
- **Consistent on the shared rails** — the same day is the same day.

## 10. Open questions

- How many **named** lives vs. **minor** lives, and how deep each tier is authored.
- How far ahead arcs are authored (a fixed multi-year script vs. rolling authored
  horizons).
- Whether — and how lightly — the world **remembers what the player witnessed**
  (recognition without turning witnessing into a mechanic).
- How much the **institution's state** (decline, rise) should bleed into the
  town's lives. The town must stay **independent**, not a mood-ring of the
  player's results (IP-002 AR) — some threads should run entirely indifferent to
  the football.
- Confirm/replace the provisional minor resident **Tomas** and the boy-and-dog arc.
