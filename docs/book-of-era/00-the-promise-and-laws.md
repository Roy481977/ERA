# THE BOOK OF ERA

## A living constitution for a world that feels like home

ERA is a place that quietly earns the player's trust until, one day, they realize it has become one of the places they call home.

This Book records what ERA is, how its world feels, what it protects and what it must never become.

It is not primarily a feature specification.

It is written as though ERA already exists, because the world must be discovered before its systems can be correctly built.

Technology may change.

Mechanics may evolve.

The Club may grow.

The district may deepen.

The soul of ERA must remain recognizable.

We do not ask first:

> Is this useful?

Or:

> Is this technically impressive?

Or even:

> Is this fun?

We first ask:

> Does this feel true?

A new part of ERA belongs in this Book when it makes us feel at least one of the following:

- I want to walk there.
- I want to meet them.
- I want to come back tomorrow.

The central measure is simple:

> After this is added, do players want to be here more?

Not because another reward is waiting.

Because the place itself has become richer.

---

# BOOK I — THE PROMISE

## Welcome home

ERA is not a football game with a decorative world around it.

ERA is a living world containing football.

The Club is its heart, but the world has weather, animals, routines, institutions, silence, memory, mystery and history of its own.

The player belongs before they manage.

They are a resident before they are an owner, strategist or optimizer.

They may open ERA with no intention of progressing.

They may simply wish to walk through the district.

To listen to the rain beneath the stadium roof.

To see whether the old dog is sleeping near the bridge.

To watch the bakery lights come on before sunrise.

To hear the evening train divide the day.

To notice that something familiar has changed.

Or to spend a quiet moment somewhere they know.

ERA does not demand attention.

It rewards attention.

It whispers rather than shouts.

It trusts the player to notice.

It permits ordinary days, private routines, half-heard conversations and changes that occur without announcement.

The world continues when unseen.

Some things grow, age, move or disappear.

Other things remain faithful.

Home is created by both continuity and change.

The impossible may exist, but without spectacle or explanation.

There are two moons.

The district has always existed beneath them.

No resident announces this.

No tutorial explains it.

No mystery system identifies it.

Children may draw both moons.

Old paintings may contain them.

Their reflections may appear together in the fountain.

A player may notice them after three minutes or after three years.

Nothing asks to be solved.

Mystery is something to live beside.

The destination is attachment.

ERA succeeds when the player no longer opens it only to discover what is new.

They open it because they miss the place.

---

# BOOK II — THE NATURE OF THE WORLD

## Somewhere just beside the real

ERA is familiar, but it is not our world.

It is a football district that has grown for more than a century somewhere just beside reality.

Close enough to feel remembered.

Different enough to remain unreachable.

Its streets, shops, weather, trains, supporters and arguments feel ordinary.

Its sky does not.

The world is warm, painterly and slightly enchanted, not because enchantment has been applied as an art style, but because the place itself is not entirely ordinary.

The light lasts a little longer than expected.

The river appears to answer the moons rather than the weather.

The Old Oak turns gold before the other trees.

The railway continues toward a distance no complete map appears to contain.

None of these things are treated as revelations.

They are simply part of the world.

ERA's mystical atmosphere must remain quiet.

It is not fantasy spectacle.

It is the feeling that the visible town rests upon something older, stranger and deeper than anyone needs to explain.

The district does not tell the player:

> There is magic here.

It allows them to feel that perhaps there is.

---

## The world never performs

The district is not a stage waiting for the player.

Morning begins without them.

The bakery warms before sunrise.

The groundskeeper inspects the pitch.

Delivery doors open.

The river moves.

The dog changes sleeping place.

Children take familiar shortcuts.

A shopkeeper moves chairs before the rain becomes heavy.

The fox completes its route after midnight whether anyone witnesses it or not.

People do not exist to present information, offer tasks or prove that the simulation is active.

They live.

The player sees fragments.

They are not shown everything.

Roughly twenty to thirty percent of the world should always remain beyond their view.

The player may shape the Club and influence the district, but they never become its sole author.

They are important.

They are not the center of existence.

---

## The district is not a map

The district is remembered through routes, landmarks, sounds and emotional associations.

The player does not primarily think:

> Go to coordinate 42, 18.

They think:

> Past the bakery, through the square, across the bridge and toward the stadium lights.

Places are learned as a resident would learn them.

The fountain.

The Oak.

The pub window.

The railway.

The museum steps.

The café bell.

The imperfect east floodlight.

The bridge at dusk.

The Club stands at the heart of the district, but the district is emotionally larger than football.

Football created much of its rhythm and identity.

Life is why the player remains.

---

## Places are older than moments

The stage comes before the actors.

Places should be designed before stories are assigned to them.

A place earns identity through accumulated life.

The Old Oak is not meaningful because the design document calls it meaningful.

It becomes meaningful because children gather beneath it, supporters touch its bark before matches, scarves remain after victories, flowers appear after deaths, the old dog sleeps beside it and generations remember different versions of the same place.

The Square is not a celebration zone.

It is where celebrations repeatedly happened until the town could no longer imagine celebrating elsewhere.

The Café is not a social hub.

It is where residents repeatedly chose to remain after matches, where arguments continued, reconciliations occurred and familiar tables became associated with familiar people.

Meaning is grown through continuity.

It is not assigned by interface labels.

---

# BOOK III — THE QUIET MYSTERIES

## Seen, never said

The deepest mysteries of ERA obey one law:

> They are seen, never said.

No resident explains them.

No child asks the obvious question for the player's benefit.

There is no lore page, tutorial, achievement, quest or collectible that names them.

They provide no gameplay advantage.

They do not determine competitive outcomes.

They are allowed to exist solely because the world deserves truths that do not belong to the player.

A mystery explained everywhere becomes a system.

ERA protects mystery from becoming content.

---

## The two moons

A smaller, quicker moon and a larger, slower moon cross the sky on different rhythms.

Their phases drift in and out of alignment.

On some nights only one is obvious.

On rare nights both are full.

On those nights, the district may seem quietly more alive.

The plaza remains full a little later.

Songs continue longer.

The pub feels warmer.

The river carries two lines of light.

No one comments on the cause.

The moons must never become a mechanic, omen, puzzle or announcement.

They are the root fact of the setting.

They make clear, without words, that ERA is not Earth.

---

## The river

The river keeps a gentle double rhythm.

Its moods follow the sky in ways that are not fully accounted for by weather.

The old bridge carries a waterline no remembered flood has crossed.

The player may notice this.

The world never confirms what it means.

---

## The Old Oak

The Old Oak is older than the district and older than the Club.

No one remembers it young.

It leans slightly toward the river.

It changes with the seasons but remains one of the world's faithful things.

It turns gold before the surrounding trees.

The groundskeeper sometimes speaks to it as if it remembers more than people do.

The Oak does not grant bonuses.

It does not unlock quests.

Its purpose is not usefulness.

Its purpose is presence, continuity and memory.

---

## The long light

Evening in ERA sometimes seems to last longer than it should.

The district exists in a near-perpetual state of almost-evening: warm windows, lengthening shadows, distant floodlights and the sense that the day is ending slowly rather than being switched off.

This atmosphere should not make every moment beautiful.

Grey mornings, rain, winter and empty Tuesdays remain essential.

The warmth matters because it coexists with ordinary life, absence and loss.

---

# BOOK IV — THE LIVING DISTRICT

## There are no NPCs

There are lives.

Residents do not exist because they are useful to the player.

They have territory, rhythms, habits, relationships, private motives and histories.

Some are central.

Some remain minor presences seen only occasionally.

Animals, birds and other forms of small life share the same world and time.

Named residents, minor residents and micro-life all move along the same persistent timeline.

Their paths cross.

They notice one another.

They change one another.

They grow older together.

The district is not filled with isolated character routines.

It is woven from interdependent lives.

---

## The old dog

The old dog belongs to no one and to the entire district.

He is not a mascot, helper, guide or quest giver.

He is simply there.

He sleeps beneath the Oak, near the bridge, outside the Club or in a patch of warm light.

Residents know him differently.

Children look for him.

Shopkeepers leave water.

Some people speak to him.

Others only notice when he is absent.

His route changes with weather, age, noise and companionship.

He may form a quiet bond with a child.

The child does not "unlock" him.

They sometimes find one another.

Sometimes they do not.

The dog ages.

His movement slows.

His sleeping places change.

One day he may no longer be there.

The district does not convert this into a quest, dramatic retention event or reward sequence.

His absence appears in routes, habits, pauses and expectations.

A bowl may remain outside a shop.

A child may continue looking near the bridge.

The Oak may feel emptier.

The loss matters because the dog was never a service.

He was alive.

---

## One shared time

All lives occur on the same persistent timeline.

An encounter witnessed by two residents is the same encounter.

A match, celebration, argument, illness, friendship or loss belongs to the history of everyone affected by it.

The world is not rebuilt separately around each character.

It has one past.

Residents may remember that past differently.

They may forget parts of it.

They may misunderstand it.

But they inhabit the same history.

This shared time is the basis of relationships, culture and tradition.

---

## Interwoven lives

Relationships are not numbers hidden beneath behavior.

They are visible through behavior.

Someone waits for a friend.

A shopkeeper reserves a familiar seat.

A child brings food for the dog.

A resident takes a longer route because they expect to meet someone.

Two people avoid each other after an argument.

A widow continues buying two coffees.

A groundskeeper greets the Oak before inspecting the pitch.

The player should learn relationships by seeing their consequences.

Not by reading affinity scores.

---

## Memory changes the future

Memory is not valuable because the simulation stores it.

Memory is valuable because tomorrow becomes different.

Victor encouraged Tomas.

Later, Victor recognizes him and stops to speak.

Two residents watched an important final together.

Later, one waits for the other before walking to the Stadium.

A child remembers that the old dog often sleeps near the bridge and checks there first.

A resident returns to a place because something meaningful happened there.

Most moments are forgotten.

Some leave traces.

Very few become part of identity.

This gradient matters.

An interaction should not automatically become lifelong memory.

The important question is:

> Will either participant carry any part of this into tomorrow?

---

## Habits create recognition

Residents should become recognizable through repeated life.

Victor often arrives early.

The baker's light reaches the street before dawn.

A musician returns to the same corner.

A supporter buys two coffees.

The dog seeks warmer stone in winter.

The twins use a shortcut adults ignore.

The florist changes the arrangement before derbies.

The player should gradually predict people.

Prediction becomes familiarity.

Familiarity becomes attachment.

---

# BOOK V — THE CLUB

## The heart, not the whole

The Club is the heart of ERA.

It shapes the district's geography, economy, routines, identity and emotional weather.

It is not the entirety of life.

Residents work, love, age, argue, raise children, tend gardens, remember the dead and care for animals beyond football.

But football passes through nearly everything.

The Club gives the week rhythm.

It gives strangers something to discuss.

It changes how the streets feel.

It creates shared hope, rivalry, pride, grief and inheritance.

Removing football would fundamentally change the district.

Removing ordinary life would destroy it.

---

## Football is lived socially

Football is not confined to matches.

It exists in:

- morning deliveries;
- academy parents;
- arguments in the Café;
- newspapers;
- scarves in windows;
- children kicking a ball against a wall;
- workers preparing the Stadium;
- supporters taking familiar routes;
- former players becoming ordinary residents;
- photographs in the Museum;
- songs heard differently after defeat;
- transfer discussions that continue for weeks.

A young academy player may become familiar before becoming famous.

A retired legend may be known through daily habits before the player understands their history.

Football history is carried by people and places, not only statistics.

---

## Matchday

On matchday, the district bends toward football.

It does not transform into a theme park.

Shops open differently.

Routes become crowded.

The Square gathers energy.

The Stadium awakens.

The groundskeeper begins earlier.

Visitors arrive by train.

Residents who rarely meet cross paths.

The dog chooses quieter streets.

After the match, the result moves outward through the district.

Victory becomes songs, scarves, crowded tables, chalk, damaged paint and tired streets the next morning.

Defeat becomes shorter conversations, early departures, closed windows and a quieter Square.

The interface may announce the score.

The world carries its consequence.

---

## Celebration and mourning

Celebration accumulates physically.

Scarves remain tied to the Oak.

Paint is marked.

Photographs appear.

Songs are repeated.

A child imitates something witnessed years earlier.

Mourning is quieter.

Flowers appear.

A black ribbon is placed in a window.

The Museum changes.

A familiar song becomes difficult to hear.

Someone buys two coffees.

A statue gathers flowers.

The world does not need to explain every gesture.

Residents understand enough.

The player is allowed to discover the rest.

---

# BOOK VI — TIME AND LIVING CONTINUITY

## ERA is built from rhythms

Days are not containers for content.

They have rhythms.

The bakery opens.

The train passes.

The fountain sounds different in winter.

Children take shortcuts.

The dog sleeps.

Rain changes movement.

The Stadium lights appear.

Shops close.

These repeated patterns create belonging.

A world becomes home because some things can be relied upon.

---

## Faithful things

Not everything should change.

The café bell remains familiar.

The fountain continues.

The Old Oak stands.

The evening train passes.

The Stadium remains visible from the bridge.

The bakery glows before sunrise.

These constants become anchors.

Change matters because it occurs around things that stayed.

If everything changes constantly, nothing can be missed.

If nothing changes, nothing is alive.

ERA depends on the tension between faithfulness and time.

---

## Slow change

Time leaves marks.

Trees grow, lose branches and recover.

Paint fades.

Murals are repaired.

Children become teenagers, academy players, workers or parents.

Animals age.

Businesses change ownership without losing all identity.

The Museum accumulates objects.

Paths appear where people repeatedly walk.

New buildings emerge only when the world has earned them.

Change should feel accumulated rather than deployed.

The player should sometimes wonder:

> Was that always there?

---

## The world notices itself

Residents respond to one another and to the physical world.

Children begin using a path created by repeated footsteps.

A shopkeeper notices that the cat has changed windows.

People move chairs before rain.

Someone notices the dog has not appeared.

A resident expects to meet another person at a familiar place.

The world must not depend on the player to recognize its changes.

It observes itself.

---

# BOOK VII — TRADITIONS

## Traditions are not thresholds

A tradition is not created because an activity occurred seventeen times.

Repeated behavior may be detected by the simulation, but repetition alone is not meaning.

A tradition forms when people remember, expect, imitate and continue a behavior together.

Victor and Elias begin meeting beneath the Oak after victories.

Others join them.

Children see it.

Years later, supporters naturally gather there.

Eventually, nobody remembers exactly when it began.

The simulation may recognize and name what has formed.

It must not manufacture tradition from statistics alone.

Tradition is shared memory hardened into culture.

---

## Tradition grows from continuity

A meaningful tradition may pass through several stages:

1. An action occurs for a reason.
2. Someone remembers it.
3. The action is repeated.
4. Other people begin to expect it.
5. Others imitate or join it.
6. The behavior becomes associated with a place, time or community.
7. Its original reason may fade while the behavior continues.

Examples:

- supporters gather in the Square before important matches;
- scarves are tied to the Oak after victories;
- residents remain at the Café after evening matches;
- children race across the bridge on Saturday mornings;
- flowers appear at the same place each year;
- a song is always begun by the same family until another generation inherits it.

Traditions must belong to people and places.

They are not achievement systems.

---

# BOOK VIII — THE INVISIBLE

ERA contains truths the player may never discover.

They exist because a world deserves depth beyond observation.

A hidden garden may be tended behind a service wall.

Its flowers appear elsewhere in the district.

The curator writes letters, some unanswered.

A florist leaves flowers for a goalkeeper who died decades ago.

A fox completes a nightly route.

Crows return to the same chimney across generations.

An old supporter buys two coffees.

The reason may never be confirmed.

These are not puzzles with missing solutions.

Puzzles ask to be solved.

Mysteries ask to be lived beside.

---

# BOOK IX — MOMENTS

Moments are the emotional atoms of ERA.

They are not necessarily missions, events or scenes.

Many are simply things that happen in a place that is alive.

## Rain beneath the Stadium roof

Rain begins while the player walks.

Umbrellas open.

The Café owner moves the chairs.

The groundskeeper continues working.

Water darkens the pitch.

The player stands beneath the roof and listens.

Nothing else happens.

The moment must not be rewarded, announced or converted into progression.

Its value is safety, stillness and presence.

## The empty Tuesday

It is a grey Tuesday.

There is no match, crisis, ceremony or new system to inspect.

People work, talk and go home.

The player walks around the Club.

Nothing remarkable happens.

It may be exactly what they needed.

ERA must allow ordinary days.

If every day is memorable, memory loses contrast.

---

# BOOK X — THE CENTRAL LAWS

## The world never performs

Life continues whether observed or not.

Missed moments remain missed.

Conversations do not repeat because the player arrived late.

## Belonging before management

The player belongs to the place before being asked to optimize it.

## Emotion before mechanics

Every mechanic must justify itself emotionally before it justifies itself mechanically.

## Attachment, not addiction

ERA earns affection.

It does not manufacture compulsion through attention traps, false urgency or constant reward pressure.

## Discovery before explanation

The world is noticed rather than narrated.

## Continuity before novelty

Identity must survive through time.

AI should sustain lives, places and relationships rather than generate endless novelty.

## Places before stories

Stories grow from geography, routines and accumulated history.

## The player shapes but does not author

The player has consequential agency inside an independent world.

They are a steward, not a sovereign.

## Mystery must remain mystery

Not everything is explained, solvable or useful.

## Ordinary life is essential

Silence, repetition, uneventful days and faithful routines are not empty space.

They are how home is formed.

---

# BOOK XI — WHAT ERA MUST NEVER BECOME

Never turn the dog into a quest giver.

Never explain the two moons.

Never notify the player whenever something subtle changes.

Never reward the player for standing in the rain.

Never make every resident useful.

Never make every mystery solvable.

Never make every day dramatic.

Never repeat missed conversations for convenience.

Never freeze beloved characters forever in order to avoid loss.

Never accelerate every slow change into content.

Never use AI to create endless novelty at the cost of identity.

Never treat silence as downtime.

Never let metrics redefine the world around what is easiest to measure.

Never let engineering elegance replace emotional truth.

Never let the simulation engine become the idea.

The engine exists to make this world possible.

---

# THE ENGINE'S PLACE

ERA is also a simulation engine.

It must be deterministic where required, persistent, observable, replayable, explainable and independent from presentation technology.

It must support:

- one shared world clock;
- persistent residents;
- routines and intentions;
- relationships;
- socially meaningful memories;
- aging;
- physical traces;
- place identity;
- shared history;
- animals and micro-life;
- matchday rhythms;
- long-term continuity;
- emergent behavior within authored boundaries.

But this engineering work represents only a small part of ERA's total creation.

The engine is the hidden structure beneath the town.

It is not the town's soul.

A technically excellent feature that makes the world feel colder, more mechanical or more artificial is not successful.

The world is upstream.

The engine must learn to carry it.

**Implementation status (honest note, updated 2026-07-19).** Current code (Sprint 1
+ Sprint 2) already serves parts of the Book: one shared clock; persistent residents
with routines/intentions; relationships and socially-meaningful memory that changes
behaviour; place identity ("their place"); shared history; matchday rhythms and
their consequences; the Old Oak's accumulating history; companionship and reunions;
observation-first tooling; determinism.

*The old dog* now partly exists (Book IV): he keeps his own daily rhythm through the
public places (the café door where water is set down, a warm patch on the square,
the shade outside the Club, and the Old Oak by evening), forms a quiet bond with the
child that deepens through repeated meetings, and ages — his movement slows and he
gives up the long walk to the Club as he grows old. He grants nothing and is not a
quest. **Not yet built for him:** his eventual death and its aftermath (the bowl
that remains, the child still looking, the Oak feeling emptier), and the shaping of
his route by weather and noise (pending weather). His ageing cadence is currently
compressed for observability and is a tuning decision, not canon.

*The world can now be watched, and the prototype has become the beginning of an
engine (DS-006).* There is now an **`Engine`**: a persistent, continuously-ticking
world you hold and advance one hour at a time, and observe through a **live
snapshot** that reads its state without changing it. The **viewer** was rebuilt as a
pure observer of those snapshots — a map on which residents and the dog move, with
per-place occupancy, the busiest gathering called out, activity callouts, an event
ticker, forming bonds, and an inspectable per-resident panel. It holds no simulation
logic; every position and count is computed in the engine, so rendering is driven
from state (per *The Engine's Place*). Determinism is preserved and asserted (two
engines ticked alike yield identical snapshots). **Honest limit:** the browser today
plays a faithful *recording* of a live run on a real-time clock — the tick loop does
not yet run *inside* the page. Doing that needs either the Rust engine compiled to
WebAssembly (currently blocked in the cloud build sandbox — the `wasm32` toolchain
can't be fetched there) or a browser-side engine mirror; this is a reserved
architecture decision (DS-006). The snapshot contract is identical either way.

*The district is becoming continuously alive (density).* A layer of ambient life now
fills the hours: the town's own routines (the ovens before dawn, the trains, the
school bell, the museum, church bells, the lamps), micro-life that moves whether or
not anyone watches (sparrows, pigeons, a cat, crows at dusk, a fox after midnight, a
cyclist over the bridge), and the residents' own small moments (a word with someone
they know, a pause at a shop window, watching the children, scratching the old dog,
pausing on the bridge). It is texture, not mechanics — it changes no world truth —
and it is deterministic. **Not yet:** most micro-life appears in the reading of the
hour rather than as moving figures on the map; weather does not yet shape any of it.

*The world is becoming observable, not just simulated (DS-007).* A **Behaviour
Layer** now sits between the simulation and the renderer: the world produces
*behaviours* — facing, walking, standing, working, talking, sitting, the dog lying
down or sniffing, birds perched — and the renderer draws those rather than prose. A
meeting is *staged* so it can be watched: two residents turn to face one another and
talk, with small gestures and a laugh, then part. The event log is demoted to a
debug side-channel. The viewer gained a follow-and-zoom camera so a conversation can
be watched up close. This is the pivot to *observation as the design tool* — and the
same behaviour stream is meant to drive a later native (Rust + Bevy) real-time
district. **Not yet:** the top-down prototype is still the only renderer; the poses
are read at a small scale until that engine exists.

*The district has animals now (DS-007).* Seven live creatures — a riverside fox, two
cats, a church owl, a grey heron, the museum crows, a hedgehog — each a persistent
entity with a character, a home range and a den, and its own day-or-night rhythm.
Several are nocturnal, so the small hours are no longer empty. Their life is grown
from a **seed** (same seed → same life, a different seed → a different one) while the
residents stay fully deterministic. **Not yet:** they express through the shared pose
vocabulary but do not yet flock/scatter as a true group, and weather does not shape
them.

Still **not yet built and future work to serve this world**: the two moons and the
sky; weather, fog and the long light; other animals / micro-life; aging, death and
loss among residents; the river's double rhythm; the hidden/invisible layer
(garden, fox, crows, the curator's letters); a fuller emotional geography (fountain,
bridge, museum, railway, pub); physical continuity of traces (scarves that remain,
litter after matchday, muddy paths, lit windows); and the ~20–30% of the world that
should always remain beyond view. No completeness is claimed; these are named so the
road stays honest.
